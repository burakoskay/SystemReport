## AMD Challenges Nvidia with Ryzen AI Halo PC

AMD released a pre-built workstation, the Ryzen AI Halo PC, priced at $3,999. This machine targets developers and enterprises running large language models locally, a market dominated by Nvidia's DGX Spark line. The Halo features AMD's newly announced Ryzen AI Max 400 chips, which integrate a dedicated AI accelerator alongside Zen 4 CPU cores. AMD's pricing undercuts Nvidia's entry-level DGX Spark configuration.

The Ryzen AI Max 400 chips represent a significant step forward in AMD's efforts to provide high-performance AI processing capabilities. By integrating a dedicated AI accelerator, AMD aims to reduce the reliance on GPU-based solutions, which have traditionally been the domain of Nvidia. The success of this strategy will depend on the performance and efficiency of the Ryzen AI Max 400 chips.

AMD's move into the AI workstation market is aggressive, with the company positioning itself as a competitor to Nvidia's established dominance. The $3,999 price point for the Ryzen AI Halo PC is a key factor in this strategy, as it offers a more affordable option for developers and enterprises looking to run large language models locally.

## Adobe Pushes AI to Desktop with Photoshop 27.7

Adobe shipped Photoshop version 27.7, adding an on-device AI model to the Remove tool. The model runs locally, reducing latency and protecting user data. Adobe notes the feature has "rather steep hardware requirements," but does not disclose exact specifications. This move by Adobe highlights the growing importance of on-device AI processing for creative professionals.

The introduction of on-device AI in Photoshop 27.7 marks a significant shift towards edge computing in the creative industry. By processing AI tasks locally, Adobe aims to improve performance and reduce reliance on cloud-based services. However, the steep hardware requirements may limit the adoption of this feature among users with lower-end hardware.

Adobe's decision to integrate on-device AI into Photoshop 27.7 reflects the company's focus on providing users with more efficient and effective tools. The Remove tool, which uses AI to automatically remove unwanted objects from images, is a prime example of this focus. By running the AI model locally, Adobe can provide users with faster and more reliable performance.
