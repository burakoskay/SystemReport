## Sci-Hub’s $15 Million Default Judgment

U.S. District Judge Robert Sweet issued a default judgment this week that orders the pirate‑academic site Sci‑Hub to pay $15 million in damages to Elsevier. The ruling follows a lawsuit filed in New York federal court nearly two years ago over the illegal distribution of Elsevier’s ScienceDirect database, which the publisher says houses “almost one‑quarter of the world’s peer‑reviewed, full‑text scientific, technical and medical content.”

Alexandra Elbakyan, the 28‑year‑old bioengineer who built Sci‑Hub, has refused to appear in court and has publicly said she will not pay the award. The site continues to operate, bouncing between new domain names in a game of “Whac‑a‑Mole” that has persisted since the case was filed. Elbakyan’s stance leaves the judgment largely symbolic; the $15 million may never be collected, but the legal precedent is clear.

The Association of American Publishers hailed the decision. President Maria Pallante said the court “has not mistaken illegal activity for a public good” and affirmed the “critical role of copyright law in furthering scientific research and the public interest.” The ruling underscores the tension between open‑access activism and the publishing industry’s business model.

## SETI Institute Receives $200 Million Endowment

The SETI Institute announced a $200 million gift from the estate of Franklin Antonio, the Qualcomm co‑founder who died on May 13, 2022. The donation, described as a “philanthropic gift,” will permanently endow core SETI programs and expand the institute’s research portfolio across six disciplines, including exoplanets, astrobiology, and the Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence.

SETI now supports more than 100 scientists working on 173 separate programs. Bill Diamond, SETI’s President and CEO, said the gift “elevates and expedites our research” and will help answer the age‑old question, “Are we alone in the universe?” Dr. Andrew Siemion noted Antonio’s technical contributions to the Allen Telescope Array, which the donor helped upgrade into a world‑class radio instrument.

Federal grants for SETI‑focused projects remain limited, forcing the institute to rely on private philanthropy. Dr. Nathalie Cabrol added that the endowment gives researchers “the freedom to pursue their own science priorities” and to explore the broader societal impact of their work.

## The Funding Paradox: Open Access vs. Private Endowments

Sci‑Hub’s illegal open‑access model and SETI’s massive private endowment sit on opposite ends of the funding spectrum. One relies on the unauthorized sharing of pay‑walled articles to democratize knowledge; the other depends on a single benefactor’s fortune to sustain research that receives scant federal support.

Both cases expose systemic gaps. Academic publishers argue that copyright protections fund the editorial and peer‑review infrastructure that underpins scholarly communication. Critics counter that the model locks up research behind paywalls, prompting sites like Sci‑Hub to emerge. Meanwhile, SETI’s reliance on philanthropy highlights the scarcity of government money for high‑risk, long‑term science.

The industry’s reaction reflects this split. Publishers celebrate the $15 million judgment as a defense of intellectual‑property rights, while the scientific community watches the SETI gift as a reminder that breakthrough discoveries often need private backing when public funds fall short.

## Earth’s Deep Oxygen Cycle: A Reminder of Why Funding Matters

A recent Ars Technica article titled “Want an oxygen‑rich atmosphere? Stuff oxygen’s friends in the mantle” points to research suggesting that carbon and sulfur dragged into Earth’s interior may be central to the planet’s oxygen budget. The study argues that mantle processes, not just surface chemistry, shape the composition of the atmosphere.

Geochemical investigations of the mantle require expensive instrumentation, deep‑drill cores, and long‑term field campaigns. Without stable funding streams, such fundamental research risks stalling, leaving gaps in our understanding of Earth’s climate history—a knowledge base that informs everything from climate models to planetary habitability studies.

The same funding challenges that drive Sci‑Hub’s illicit distribution also constrain the laboratories that study Earth’s interior. Whether through public grants, private endowments, or controversial open‑access platforms, the flow of money determines which scientific questions get answered.

## What to Watch

The next steps for Sci‑Hub will likely involve appeals and possible enforcement actions aimed at seizing domain registrars. Keep an eye on whether the $15 million judgment spurs stricter anti‑piracy measures or prompts legislative proposals to protect open‑access advocates. For SETI, track the rollout of new missions funded by Antonio’s endowment, especially any upgrades to the Allen Telescope Array and collaborations with NASA, the Department of Energy, and the National Science Foundation. The juxtaposition of a courtroom‑driven penalty and a philanthropic windfall will continue to shape the debate over how science is funded and shared.
