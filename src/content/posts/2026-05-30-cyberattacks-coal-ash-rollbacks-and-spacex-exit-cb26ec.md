## Hackers Claim Data Theft from MyPillow

A ransomware group announced it obtained sensitive data from MyPillow, the bedding brand owned by Mike Lindell. The claim emerged as part of a broader trend of cybercriminals targeting political figures and their businesses. While the group hasn’t provided evidence, similar tactics have been used in high-profile breaches over the past year.

The attackers described the incident as a "proof of concept" to pressure Lindell, who has been a vocal supporter of former President Trump and the Stop the Steal movement. MyPillow has not publicly commented on the alleged breach, though cybersecurity experts warn that unverified claims from ransomware groups often lack substance. Previous attacks on Lindell’s entities have been linked to state-sponsored actors, but attribution remains speculative without forensic analysis.

## Danish Pension Fund Dumps SpaceX

A major Danish pension fund has excluded SpaceX from its portfolio, citing governance and valuation concerns. The decision, announced in a Reuters report, reflects growing skepticism among institutional investors about Elon Musk’s leadership style and the company’s financial sustainability. The fund, which manages billions in assets, stated it would not reevaluate the exclusion in the near term.

This move follows similar actions by other investors who question SpaceX’s ability to monetize its satellite internet ambitions and maintain regulatory compliance. Musk’s track record of delayed milestones and aggressive expansion has raised red flags, particularly as the space industry faces increasing scrutiny over debris and safety protocols. The exclusion could signal a shift in institutional confidence toward more conventional aerospace firms.

## Trump’s Coal Ash Rollbacks Face Legal Challenges

The Trump administration’s proposed coal ash rule changes, which would hand oversight to states and allow them to bypass federal standards, have drawn sharp criticism from environmental groups and scientists. Current regulations require power plants to treat coal ash as hazardous waste, but the new framework would let states decide their own monitoring and enforcement protocols.

Activists argue the rollbacks ignore decades of research linking coal ash to health risks like cancer and birth defects. The American Lung Association called the policy a "dangerous gamble" that could harm communities near coal facilities. Legal challenges are expected, with environmental organizations preparing lawsuits to block implementation before the end of 2024.

## Industry Context: Cybersecurity and Energy Policy

The MyPillow incident highlights a persistent challenge in cybersecurity: the intersection of political activism and digital targets. Lindell’s companies have long been in the crosshairs of both state and non-state actors, making them test cases for how private entities manage high-risk exposure. The attack also underscores a broader issue—many small businesses lack the resources to defend against sophisticated hacking attempts.

Meanwhile, the coal ash debate reflects a larger tension between regulatory centralization and state autonomy. Environmental advocates argue that federal standards are necessary to prevent a patchwork of lax rules, while industry groups claim uniform regulations stifle innovation. The outcome of this fight may set a precedent for future energy policy battles, particularly as renewable energy adoption accelerates.

## What to Watch Next

The coming months will test how these issues evolve. For MyPillow, a public response or forensic audit could clarify the breach’s validity. For SpaceX, the pension fund’s exclusion might prompt a reevaluation of corporate governance practices. In energy policy, the legal fate of the coal ash rule will determine whether states gain more leeway or face federal pushback. Each of these threads will shape their respective industries in the year ahead.
