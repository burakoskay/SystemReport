Meta released Forum, a dedicated iPhone app that consolidates Facebook Groups into a single feed and layers two AI tools on top. The move signals Meta’s attempt to give groups their own surface while testing AI‑driven discovery.

The app appeared in the App Store without a press release, flagged by Matt Navarra of the Geekout Newsletter. Its store listing calls it “a dedicated space for the conversations that matter most to you.” Users must log in with their Facebook credentials; the app then imports every group the account belongs to and populates a feed with those posts. A short onboarding prompt asks what topics the user wants to see more of, after which the feed also surfaces suggestions from groups the user has not joined. The rollout is currently described as a public test, according to a Meta spokesperson.

## Dedicated space for group conversations

Forum replaces the generic Facebook home feed with a view that shows only group activity. Posts appear in chronological order, and the app does not mix in friend updates, page posts, or algorithmic suggestions that dominate the main app. This design forces the conversation to stay within the boundaries of each group’s community.

The feed also offers a discovery pane that recommends groups based on the user’s stated interests. Unlike the main Facebook app, the recommendations are limited to other groups, not pages or advertisers. The app respects the existing anonymity options from Facebook: users can post under a pseudonym that the main platform already supports, but group administrators retain the ability to see the real account behind each post.

## AI‑driven question answering and moderation

The first AI feature, labeled “Ask,” scans the user’s entire group roster for relevant answers when a question is typed. Instead of opening each group manually, the tool aggregates responses that have already been posted elsewhere on the platform. Meta markets the feature as a way to avoid “searching groups one by one,” and the early test suggests it can surface answers within seconds.

A second AI tool acts as an admin assistant. It surfaces moderation cues, flags potentially problematic content, and proposes actions for group managers. The assistant is intended to reduce the manual workload of moderators who oversee large communities. When asked about the feature, a Meta spokesperson said the company “tests lots of new products publicly to see what people find interesting and useful across our apps.” The statement underscores that both AI tools are still experimental.

## History and competitive context

Meta is not new to standalone group experiences. The company launched a separate Groups app in the early 2010s, only to retire it in 2017 after low adoption. That failure is often cited as a cautionary tale for any effort to split Facebook’s core social graph into a niche product.

Reddit occupies the space that Forum seems to target: a platform where users seek “real answers” from “real people.” Unlike Reddit, Forum ties every interaction to a Facebook identity, even when users post under a pseudonym. The requirement to log in with a Facebook account eliminates the truly anonymous posting model that Reddit relies on, and it gives Meta a direct line to user data that Reddit does not have.

## Privacy and moderation trade‑offs

Forum inherits Facebook’s existing privacy framework, but the combination of group‑centric feeds and AI assistance raises new questions. The AI scans content across all groups a user belongs to, potentially aggregating data that was previously siloed. While Meta has not published a separate privacy notice for Forum, the broader platform’s data‑use policies still apply.

Administrators can view the real identities behind pseudonymous posts, a fact that could deter some users from participating openly. At the same time, the admin assistant’s automated flags may surface content that users consider benign. The balance between automated moderation and community self‑governance remains unsettled, and Meta’s public testing phase will likely surface friction points.

## What to watch

The next few weeks will reveal whether Forum graduates from a public test to a full release. Key signals include iOS update notes, changes to the App Store description, and any shift in Meta’s messaging about the AI features. Watch for statements from privacy regulators, especially if the AI “Ask” tool is found to cross‑reference data in ways that trigger compliance concerns. Finally, monitor Reddit’s response; a feature rollout that draws active group members away could prompt competitive tweaks on that platform.
