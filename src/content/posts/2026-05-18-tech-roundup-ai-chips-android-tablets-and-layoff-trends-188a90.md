## The AI Chip Hoarding War

Meta, Tesla, and venture capital firm Andreessen Horowitz are among the companies stockpiling Nvidia's highly sought-after H100 GPUs, a crucial component in training AI models. Each H100 can cost up to $40,000, and one big tech company, Meta, has amassed 350,000 of them. According to a research paper accompanying the release of Meta's Llama 3.1 model, the 405b parameter version was trained using up to 16,000 of Nvidia's popular H100 GPUs. This amounts to a staggering $640 million worth of hardware.

The demand for H100s is so high that people are being paid to sneak them into China to bypass U.S. export controls. OpenAI has also secured a special relationship with Nvidia, with the CEO Jensen Huang hand-delivering the first cluster of Nvidia's next-generation H200 GPUs.

The AI chip market has become increasingly competitive, with companies willing to pay top dollar for these highly sought-after components. This has led to a surge in demand, with some companies even resorting to extreme measures to get their hands on these chips.

## Android Tablets Get a Boost

In a more affordable tech space, Walmart has launched six new Android 16 tablets under its Onn brand, starting at $97. These tablets appear surprisingly decent on paper, offering a budget-friendly option for consumers. Meanwhile, Motorola has quietly released the Moto Tag 2, a device with UWB support in Android Find Hub and longer battery life.

The Android tablet market has seen significant growth in recent years, with companies like Walmart and Motorola offering affordable options for consumers. The launch of these new tablets is expected to further increase competition in the market.

## The Dark Side of Layoffs

A concerning trend in the job market has emerged, where companies are reposting jobs at significantly lower salaries following layoffs. Career coach Mandy took to TikTok to shed light on this practice, which has sparked widespread conversation. Imagine working at a big tech company for five or six years, earning a salary of $180,000, only to be laid off and have the job reposted for $140,000. This tactic allows companies to reset the salary base to a much lower threshold.

The trend of reposting jobs at lower salaries has significant implications for the job market, with many employees feeling undervalued and underpaid. This trend is expected to continue, with companies looking for ways to cut costs in a competitive market.
