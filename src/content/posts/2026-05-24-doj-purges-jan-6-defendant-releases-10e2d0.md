## DOJ Wipes Jan. 6 Defendant Releases

The Associated Press reported that the DOJ removed news releases mentioning individuals charged in the Jan. 6 Capitol attack from its public website. The deletions raise questions about digital preservation standards within federal agencies and whether similar clean-ups could affect other politically sensitive topics. Critics argue that the move undermines transparency and hampers research on the government's own prosecutions. This move has sparked concerns among media outlets and researchers, who rely on these releases to track and analyze the government's actions.

## Cannabis Climbs to Schedule III

Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche announced that products containing marijuana will move from Schedule I to Schedule III, placing them on par with codeine-combined Tylenol. The reclassification applies to products covered by the FDA or those holding a state medical-marijuana licence. Blanche also called for a June hearing to consider moving all marijuana to a lower schedule. This move marks a significant shift in the country's drug policy and could have far-reaching implications for the cannabis industry.

The rescheduling of cannabis is seen as a major victory for advocates who have long argued that the current classification is too restrictive and does not reflect the plant's medical benefits. However, some critics argue that the move does not go far enough and that marijuana should be fully legalized.

The cannabis industry is likely to experience significant changes as a result of this reclassification. For example, companies may need to adapt their products and marketing strategies to comply with new regulations. Additionally, the reclassification could lead to increased investment in the industry, as more investors become confident in the legitimacy of cannabis products.

## eBay Faces DOJ EPA-Related Lawsuit

The DOJ filed a complaint accusing eBay of facilitating the sale of products that violate EPA regulations. eBay responded with a statement emphasizing its efforts to block and remove more than 99.9% of offending listings. The lawsuit will likely shape how digital platforms are held accountable for third-party listings that run afoul of environmental regulations. This move is part of a broader effort by the government to crack down on online marketplaces that facilitate the sale of illicit or harmful products.

The lawsuit against eBay is significant because it highlights the challenges of regulating online marketplaces and ensuring that they comply with federal laws. The case could set an important precedent for how the government approaches these issues in the future.

The eBay lawsuit also raises questions about the responsibility of online marketplaces to monitor and control third-party listings. While eBay has emphasized its efforts to remove offending listings, some critics argue that more needs to be done to prevent the sale of harmful products.
