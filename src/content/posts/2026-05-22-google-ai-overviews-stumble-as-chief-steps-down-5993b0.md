## Leadership shakeup and AI Overviews
Google announced that John Giannandrea is leaving his dual role as head of AI and search. Giannandrea joined Google in 2010 and expanded his remit to include search last year. The company says he will stay to work directly on technology. Jeff Dean, who has led the Google Brain project since 1999, will inherit Giannandrea’s AI responsibilities. Ben Gomes, currently VP of search engineering, will become the new search chief.
The reshuffle consolidates Google Brain and other AI projects under a single umbrella. Giannandrea’s public profile has been low, so the move caught many analysts off guard. In a 2023 interview, he dismissed AI apocalypse hype, saying, “I’m definitely not worried about the AI apocalypse.” The statement underscores his pragmatic view of AI deployment.
The timing aligns with Google’s aggressive rollout of AI Overviews, the answer boxes that sit above organic results. The feature now appears in more countries and on a broader set of queries than when it launched. The leadership change raises questions about how Google will address the growing criticism of those overviews.

## Overviews trip on simple language
Google’s AI Overviews break when users include the word “disregard” in a query. The glitch was highlighted by a 9to5Google report that noted the system fails to parse the term. The error is symptomatic of broader reliability issues.
In a test query on Friday, the overview responded, “No, today is not Friday. Today is Friday, May 30, 2025.” The answer was both contradictory and factually wrong. The system offered no citation or expert backing for the claim.
These mistakes matter because Overviews sit at the top of the SERP, pushing organic links down. Users who trust the AI snippet may never see the underlying web pages that originally earned Google its relevance reputation.

## User backlash and DIY fixes
A growing number of users have turned to browser extensions to hide the AI Overviews. One developer released “Bye, Bye Google AI,” which now has 41,000 installs. The extension works in Chrome, Edge, and other Chromium‑based browsers, with a Firefox version in development.
Version 1.5 adds support for 19 languages, including English, French, German, Spanish, Korean, Japanese, Mandarin (both scripts), Arabic, Hebrew, Urdu, Hindi, Thai, Greek, Italian, Polish, Russian, Dutch, Danish, and Portuguese. It also lets users hide the AI Mode tab, not just the overview.
A simpler, albeit manual, workaround is to append “-noai” to any search string. Adding the parameter “&udm=14” forces Google to show the traditional web tab without AI elements. Both tricks require users to remember extra characters, limiting their adoption.

## Technical and strategic implications
The Overviews rely on large language models that ingest the open web for training data. Critics argue that the same content creators who fuel Google’s ad revenue are now being used to train the AI without direct compensation. The tension could pressure Google to rethink its data pipeline.
An internal email from December 26, 2018 predicted that deep machine‑learning systems would soon outperform Google’s two‑decade relevance algorithms. The writer cited BERT as the catalyst that already displaced earlier methods. If that prediction holds, AI Overviews may become the default ranking signal, reducing the role of traditional relevance signals.
Jeff Dean’s new oversight of AI could accelerate integration of LLMs into core search. However, the recent glitches suggest that engineering rigor has not kept pace with product rollout. The balance between rapid feature deployment and reliability will shape Google’s competitive edge.

## What to watch
Track how Jeff Dean’s AI team addresses the “disregard” bug and similar parsing failures. Monitor whether Google expands the “&udm=14” parameter into a user‑facing toggle. Watch Ben Gomes’s early moves as search chief, especially any changes to the placement of AI Overviews. The next quarterly earnings call should reveal whether Google plans to scale back the AI snippet or double down on its integration.
