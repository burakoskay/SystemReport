## Surveillance Program Renewal Sparks Concerns

The US government is pushing to renew a surveillance program that allows the FBI to view Americans' communications without a warrant. The program, which is set to expire, has sparked concerns among lawmakers and civil liberties groups.

## Program Details

The program in question is Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA), which was first introduced in 2008. It allows the FBI to collect communications from foreigners outside the US, but also incidentally collects communications from Americans who are in contact with those foreigners. The program has been used to thwart terrorist plots, but critics argue that it can be abused.

## Lawmaker Concerns

A new bill aims to address mounting lawmaker concerns about the program, but critics argue that it does not go far enough to protect Americans' civil liberties. The bill would extend the program for another five years, but with some modifications to the oversight process. Lawmakers have expressed concerns about the lack of transparency and accountability in the program, as well as the potential for abuse.

## History of Surveillance Programs

The use of surveillance programs by the US government dates back to the 1970s, when the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA) was first introduced. The act established procedures for the government to obtain warrants to conduct surveillance on foreign targets. However, the act has been amended several times since then, including the introduction of Section 702 in 2008. The program has been the subject of controversy and debate, with some arguing that it is necessary for national security and others arguing that it infringes on civil liberties.
