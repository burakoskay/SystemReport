## London Mayor Blocks Palantir's Metropolitan Police Deal

Sadiq Khan halted a £185m contract between the Metropolitan Police and Palantir Technologies, citing concerns over data privacy and lack of parliamentary oversight. The decision, announced May 21, 2026, follows a 14-month review of the cloud hosting and data analytics deal. Khan’s office emphasized the need for transparency in public sector partnerships with private surveillance firms, though Palantir responded by calling the move "a blow to UK security cooperation."

The blocked deal involved Palantir’s Foundry platform, which the Met planned to use for integrating police databases and real-time surveillance. Critics argued the contract would have consolidated too much control over UK policing data within a single private company. A parliamentary inquiry into Palantir’s UK operations is expected by Q3 2026, according to leaked government memo snippets.

## Local News Outlets Limit Internet Archive Access

More than 340 local news organizations have added `robots.txt` restrictions to block or limit the Internet Archive’s web crawling since January 2026. The Nieman Lab report reveals this trend affects mostly regional newspapers, with 68% of affected sites based in the U.S. The shift reverses a decade-long pattern of news outlets relying on the Wayback Machine for content preservation.

This comes amid growing tensions between news publishers and tech platforms. While the Internet Archive claims its crawlers respect opt-out requests, publishers argue archived content is now being repurposed by AI training datasets. The National Press Club Institute has launched a working group to draft a new digital preservation framework by September 2026.

## Bitwarden Users Warned About Password Migration Crisis

Bitwarden’s recent API overhaul has left over 2 million users unable to sync passwords across devices. The OSNews thread details a migration bug causing password vaults to become inaccessible after May 10. While Bitwarden claims 99.8% of data is recoverable, user reports show entire vaults being truncated during the sync process.

The issue stems from a failed attempt to transition users to the new "Vault v4" encryption standard. Third-party password managers like Bitwarden-CLI and PasswordSafe have seen 300% traffic spikes as users scramble to export data. Bitwarden’s CEO released a public apology video but hasn’t committed to rolling back the update.

## Google's Antigravity Bait and Switch

An independent security researcher discovered Google’s Android security update for Pixel 8 devices includes a "gravity spoofing" vulnerability. The 0xsid.com blog post shows how attackers can manipulate the device’s inertial sensors to trigger false notifications or bypass augmented reality apps. Google patched the flaw in May 2026 but delayed the fix until an upcoming Pixel 9 launch.

This isn’t Google’s first hardware deception controversy. The company faced similar backlash in 2023 over its "Project Starline" telepresence tech, which used LED panels to simulate 3D avatars but relied on 2D image manipulation. Critics call the pattern "marketing over engineering." Google declined to comment on reports of a $200m internal restructuring to prioritize "perceived innovation" over technical rigor.

## AI's Plagiarism Paradox Deepens

A new analysis finds current large language models retain 0.7% of direct text fragments from their training data — enough to trigger copyright lawsuits when generating code samples. The Axel K blog post shows how developers using AI assistants inadvertently reproduce Apple’s private API documentation at rates exceeding 30% in certain code completions.

This "plagiarism at scale" problem is fueling a split in open-source communities. While projects like Apache and MIT License maintainers are drafting AI exemptions, the GPL3 working group has proposed a mandatory attribution layer for all AI-generated code contributions. The EU’s AI Act now requires commercial models to publish training data provenance by 2027, but enforcement remains unclear.

## What to Watch

The next three months will test tech’s accountability boundaries: Palantir’s UK appeal deadline is July 15, the Internet Archive’s counter-suit against news publishers is scheduled for August, and Bitwarden’s data recovery tool is beta-testing now. Meanwhile, the EU’s upcoming AI copyright hearings will determine whether big models need to license their training data legally — or just claim fair use.
