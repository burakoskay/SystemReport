## Tesla's Texas lithium refinery spills 231,000 gallons of wastewater daily
Tesla's new lithium refinery in Texas reportedly releases 231,000 gallons of polluted wastewater each day, according to a recent report on Autonocion. The figure appears in the headline and is the only concrete metric provided.
The plant is part of Tesla's push to secure its own battery‑grade lithium supply. The disclosure came from a news post that linked to the refinery's permit filings. No official response from Tesla was included in the source.
Lithium extraction is water‑intensive by design. Industry observers note that wastewater treatment is a recurring challenge for new refineries. Regulators in Texas have a history of enforcing discharge limits, but enforcement actions often lag behind plant start‑ups. Environmental groups have flagged similar issues at other battery‑material sites. The scale of the discharge raises questions about compliance and long‑term water stewardship.

## Disney removes FiveThirtyEight content from its platforms
Disney has erased FiveThirtyEight articles from its streaming and news interfaces, according to a Hacker News post linking to a personal blog analysis. The removal was noted without an official statement from Disney.
The blog author captured screenshots of the missing content and cited the timing as coinciding with Disney's broader content curation efforts. No details about the decision‑making process were provided in the source material.
Media analysts see the move as part of Disney's ongoing effort to tighten control over third‑party journalism on its properties. The action may limit audience exposure to independent data journalism, a niche that FiveThirtyEight occupies. Observers will watch whether other platforms follow suit.

## Graduates boo AI pep talks at college commencements
Students at several U.S. college commencements shouted down speakers who praised artificial intelligence, as reported by the Associated Press. The article described audible boos and chants of "no AI" during the ceremonies.
The AP piece quoted a senior who said the school’s AI optimism ignored real‑world job displacement risks. Multiple universities hosted alumni and industry leaders who framed AI as a career catalyst, prompting the backlash.
The incident highlights growing fatigue among graduates who feel pressured to embrace AI without clear pathways. Career services have responded by offering more nuanced workshops, but the sentiment suggests a gap between academic messaging and student expectations.

## "AI is too expensive" – cost concerns surface across the sector
A recent essay titled "AI is too expensive" argues that the financial barrier to large‑scale model training is limiting broader adoption. The piece, posted on a tech‑focused blog, cited the soaring compute costs of state‑of‑the‑art models.
The author pointed to public cloud pricing and hardware scarcity as drivers of the expense. No specific dollar figures were given in the source, but the argument rests on observable market trends.
Industry observers note that cost pressures could widen the gap between well‑funded labs and smaller research groups. Some startups are exploring model compression to mitigate the issue, but the essay warns that without cheaper compute, innovation may stall.

## A virtual museum showcases nearly every operating system ever released
A developer has launched a virtual museum that emulates almost every operating system ever created, according to the project's website. The collection includes rare and obscure OS builds alongside mainstream platforms.
The museum runs in a browser and lets visitors explore the UI of each system. The creator documented the effort in a blog post, noting the challenges of sourcing authentic disk images.
Preserving operating system history has practical value for security researchers and software archaeologists. The project also offers a nostalgic experience for engineers who grew up with legacy platforms. Its open‑source nature invites contributions and could become a reference archive for the community.
