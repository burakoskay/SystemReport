Marathon’s first open play week drops on June 2, and the gaming press is already bruised by a wave of layoffs.

Bungie confirmed the open play week and teased new season details, while a free Alan Wake update now haunts Phasmophobia on every platform. At the same time, Vox Media’s Polygon was sold to Valnet, and veteran staff were let go in a surprise move that many see as a symptom of broader media decay.

## Marathon opens play week June 2

The open play week gives players a chance to test the upcoming season before it locks. Bungie said the week will start on June 2 and will showcase new activities, loot, and progression paths. The company also shared more information about what players can expect from the game’s new season, but stopped short of revealing specific numbers or dates beyond the start.

For players, the open play week is a rare window to shape the season’s balance. It also provides Bungie with real‑time data to tweak difficulty and rewards. The timing aligns with the industry’s push to keep live services fresh, a strategy that has paid off for shooters and MMOs alike.

## Alan Wake haunts Phasmophobia

A free update now places the horror writer Alan Wake inside the ghost‑hunting game Phasmophobia. The crossover lands on all platforms without cost, turning a co‑op terror experience into a narrative mash‑up.

The update adds Wake‑themed skins, voice lines, and a new “Possessed Manuscript” objective. Players must locate the manuscript while avoiding the usual spectral threats. By borrowing a recognizable IP, the developers hope to attract fans of the Alan Wake series and boost Phasmophobia’s already strong community.

## Polygon sold, staff cut

Vox Media sold Polygon to Valnet, the owner of dozens of internet content brands. Valnet reports over 260 million page views across its portfolio. The acquisition triggered immediate layoffs of veteran journalists, including co‑founder and editor‑in‑chief Chris Plante and senior writer Michael McWhertor.

Polygon co‑founder Brian Crecente posted on Bluesky, “just completely sickened by this news.” He lamented the impact on staff and the dwindling number of publications seriously covering video games. Co‑founder Chris Plante and senior writer Michael McWhertor were among those whose contracts were terminated without warning.

The layoffs unfolded in real time. Polygon senior reporter Nicole Carpenter published a story about the Epic vs. Apple case at 10 a.m. Eastern, then announced her own layoff less than two hours later on Bluesky. “Along with just about everyone else at Polygon, I am now out of a job, ending over a decade at Vox Media for me,” Curation Editor Pete Volk wrote.

Valnet’s founder Hassan Youssef previously worked at Mansef Inc., the company behind Brazzers and a former minority investor in the firm that ran Pornhub. Critics have accused Valnet of low pay for long articles, but the company sued The Wrap in federal court, defending its reputation as a supporter of high‑quality journalism.

Vox Media’s CEO Jim Bankoff issued a statement praising Polygon’s legacy, but the tone of the announcement did little to soothe the displaced staff. The rapid transition underscores how consolidation can upend even well‑established gaming outlets.

## Media enshittification and the lone survivor

The term “enshittification,” coined by journalist Cory Doctorow, describes how attention‑driven platforms degrade content quality in pursuit of ad revenue. Blue’s News, a 30‑year‑old site founded by Stephen “Blue” Heaslip, positions itself as a counterpoint.

Heaslip’s site began as a Quake hype blog in 1996 and now aggregates PC gaming and tech news with no AI‑generated filler or sponsored posts. “No AI slop, no sponsored posts. Sorry, not sorry,” the site’s tagline reads. Heaslip says the site is “pure function and no filler,” a stance he believes helps stem the tide of enshittification.

Blue’s News persists with a layout that feels like a “yellowing Packard Bell CRT,” according to Heaslip. The site’s minimalism stands in stark contrast to the sprawling, ad‑laden networks that dominate the market. While larger outlets chase clicks, Blue’s News offers concise, neutral roundups that appeal to readers fatigued by clickbait.

The survival of Blue’s News highlights a niche for ad‑free, community‑focused journalism. As corporate owners like Valnet expand, independent sites may become the last bastions of quality coverage, provided they can sustain themselves without the economies of scale that larger networks enjoy.

## What to watch

Track Valnet’s handling of Polygon over the next six months. Will the brand retain its editorial voice or succumb to the cost‑cutting model that has plagued other acquisitions? Monitor Marathon’s season rollout for balance changes that could set a benchmark for live‑service iteration. Keep an eye on Phasmophobia’s player metrics after the Alan Wake update to see if cross‑IP collaborations become a new growth engine. Finally, watch whether Blue’s News can scale its ad‑free model or remain a solitary outpost in a sea of enshittified platforms.
