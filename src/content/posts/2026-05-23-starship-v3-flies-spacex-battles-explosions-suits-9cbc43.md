SpaceX’s Starship V3 completed its inaugural test flight, hitting most of its objectives.

The flight lifted off from Boca Chica, Texas, and the company reported that the majority of its pre‑flight checklist was satisfied, according to Engadget. The test marked the first powered attempt of the third‑generation vehicle, which SpaceX bills as larger, more powerful, and capable of on‑orbit docking.

### A string of pressure‑test mishaps

Just weeks before the V3 flight, an upgraded Starship booster exploded during a gas‑system pressure test. Livestreamers captured a blast that ripped out an entire side of the lower booster, even though no engines were attached. SpaceX later said the test was meant to verify gas‑system pressures and that the area had been cleared, leaving no injuries. The incident underscored how early‑stage hardware can fail dramatically when internal pressures exceed design limits.

A similar failure occurred earlier this year when the SN3 prototype collapsed during cryogenic proof testing. Video from a Boca Chica observer showed the fuselage crumpling under simulated flight‑level pressures. Elon Musk later suggested the collapse might have stemmed from a test‑configuration error rather than a flaw in the vehicle itself. Both episodes remind engineers that even a single faulty valve or sensor can derail a schedule that is already aggressive.

### Legal friction on the Texas coast

While the rockets roar, the surrounding community is fighting back. The Sierra Club, the Carrizo/Comecrudo Tribe of Texas, and the nonprofit Save RGV have sued the Texas General Land Office, land commissioner George P. Bush, and Cameron County over periodic closures of Boca Chica Beach. The plaintiffs argue that the 2013 amendment to the Texas Open Beaches Act—passed to accommodate space‑flight operations—conflicts with the 2009 constitutional amendment that guarantees free beach access. According to the Sierra Club, the beach was shut for roughly 196 hours in the first quarter of 2022 and over 600 hours in 2021, effectively denying locals and tribal members their historic shoreline.

The lawsuit, slated for a June 1 hearing, does not name SpaceX as a defendant, but the company’s testing cadence is the de‑facto cause of the restrictions. The legal challenge could force Texas officials to reevaluate how they balance aerospace ambitions with public‑access rights, potentially adding another regulatory hurdle for Starship’s path to orbit.

### Heat‑shield experiments and the race to reusability

In a quieter corner of SpaceX’s program, the Cargo Dragon capsule C108 carried a handful of ceramic Starship heat‑shield tiles on its CRS‑18 mission. The tiles, placed near the leading edge of the shield, will experience re‑entry heating and return intact for post‑flight inspection. This flight‑test approach leverages Dragon’s proven reusability to validate a critical component of Starship’s thermal protection system without risking the full vehicle.

The move is pragmatic, but it also highlights how much work remains. Starship must demonstrate that its heat‑shield system can survive repeated orbital re‑entries with minimal refurbishment if the company hopes to drive down launch costs. Meanwhile, Blue Origin has accelerated its own mega‑rocket development, recently flying New Glenn twice and unveiling a larger booster design that directly competes with Starship’s payload capacity. NASA’s acting administrator, Sean Duffy, has already hinted at opening the lunar‑landing contract to competitors if SpaceX’s schedule slips.

### What to watch next

The next milestone for Starship V3 is a full‑duration orbital flight, which will require a successful in‑orbit fuel transfer—a capability NASA demands before approving crewed lunar missions. Observers should track the outcome of the upcoming June 1 beach‑access lawsuit, the timeline for the gas‑system pressure‑test investigation, and any public statements from Elon Musk about the schedule for the orbital test. Each of these variables will shape whether Starship can stay on track for a 2028 Moon landing or be forced to cede ground to Blue Origin’s New Glenn.
