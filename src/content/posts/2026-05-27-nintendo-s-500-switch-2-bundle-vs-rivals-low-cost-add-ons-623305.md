Nintendo slapped a $500 price tag on its Switch 2 bundle, tacking a first‑party game onto the console just as the standalone unit climbs in September. Early adopters now face a choice: pay the premium now or wait for a price dip that may never come.

Engadget notes the bundle arrives while Nintendo’s base Switch 2 price is set to rise later this month, turning the bundle into a potential cash‑save for fans who were already planning to buy the new hardware. The bundle’s headline price of $500 includes the console, dock, Joy‑Cons, and an unnamed first‑party title, but it does not disclose any discount on the game itself.

## iPadOS 26.5 Leverages Magic Accessories

iPadOS 26.5 rolled out earlier this month, and 9to5Mac highlights a quiet upgrade: the OS now lets a Magic Keyboard, Magic Trackpad, or Magic Mouse control an iPhone or iPad when paired. The change is under‑the‑radar, but it blurs the line between macOS peripherals and iOS devices, giving power users a desktop‑like workflow without a laptop.

The update does not add new hardware; it simply expands Bluetooth hand‑off and cursor support. For developers, the shift means apps can assume a mouse or trackpad will be present, potentially reshaping UI conventions that have long catered to touch‑first interactions. Critics may argue the move favors Apple’s own accessories over third‑party options, but the practical benefit for anyone already owning a Magic Keyboard is immediate.

## Google Home’s $35 Outdoor Camera Expands Gemini Program

Walmart quietly launched its third Google Home camera this week, a wired outdoor unit priced at $35, according to 9to5Google. The camera joins the “Gemini built‑in” program, which bundles Google’s AI‑enhanced video analytics with low‑cost hardware.

At $35 the unit undercuts most competing outdoor cameras, which typically start near $100. The price point suggests Walmart is betting on volume and the Gemini brand’s ability to turn cheap sensors into useful security data. The camera’s wired design limits placement flexibility, but the trade‑off is a simple power solution that avoids battery‑swap headaches.

## Samsung Phones Offer CLEAR‑Powered Digital US Passport

Samsung has partnered with CLEAR to embed a digital US passport option directly into Galaxy phones, 9to5Google reports. The feature mirrors CLEAR’s existing airport verification system, allowing travelers to scan a phone‑based credential instead of a physical passport.

The integration is limited to U.S. citizens and works only at airports that already accept CLEAR’s biometric checks. While the rollout is modest, it signals a broader push to make smartphones the primary identity token for high‑security contexts. Privacy advocates will watch how Samsung stores and transmits biometric data, but the convenience factor for frequent flyers is undeniable.

## Valve’s Steam Deck Returns, Yet Price Stings

After months of scarcity, Valve’s four‑year‑old Steam Deck is back in stock, but Ars Technica warns the price tag feels “unfortunately modern.” The handheld now sits at a price point that rivals many mid‑range laptops, eroding the value proposition that once made it a niche success.

The restock reveals a tension between supply chain realities and consumer expectations. Valve’s hardware team has not announced a price cut, and the market’s appetite for portable PC gaming appears strong enough to sustain the higher cost. Competitors may seize the moment to launch cheaper alternatives, but the Deck’s library and Steam ecosystem remain its strongest selling points.

## What to Watch Next

Track Nintendo’s September price adjustment; a larger hike could make the $500 bundle look like a bargain, while a modest increase might render it moot. Monitor iPadOS updates for deeper peripheral integration, especially any API changes that affect third‑party keyboards. Keep an eye on Walmart’s sales data for the Gemini camera to see if low‑cost AI surveillance gains traction. Samsung’s CLEAR passport will need regulatory clearance beyond airports before it becomes a mainstream identity tool. Finally, watch Valve’s pricing strategy—if the Deck’s cost climbs further, we may see a new class of affordable handhelds emerge to fill the gap.
