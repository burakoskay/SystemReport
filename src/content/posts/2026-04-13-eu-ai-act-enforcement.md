The European Union's landmark AI Act has officially entered its enforcement phase, marking the beginning of the world's most comprehensive regulatory framework for artificial intelligence. Companies operating in the EU now face concrete compliance requirements — and the clock is ticking.

## Risk-Based Classification

The Act categorizes AI systems into four risk tiers: unacceptable, high, limited, and minimal. Systems deemed "unacceptable risk" — such as social scoring or real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces — are now outright banned, with limited exceptions for law enforcement.

High-risk systems, which include AI used in hiring, credit scoring, healthcare diagnosis, and critical infrastructure, must meet stringent requirements:

- **Transparency**: Users must be informed when they're interacting with AI
- **Human oversight**: Meaningful human review must be possible at all decision points
- **Data quality**: Training data must be documented and tested for bias
- **Robustness**: Systems must be tested against adversarial attacks and edge cases

## Compliance Timeline

While the full enforcement ramps up over the next 18 months, several provisions are already active. Companies deploying general-purpose AI models (like large language models) must register their systems and provide technical documentation about capabilities and limitations.

The penalties for non-compliance are severe: up to 7% of global annual revenue for the most serious violations, compared to GDPR's 4% maximum.

## Industry Response

The tech industry's response has been mixed. European startups have largely embraced the framework, viewing it as a competitive advantage that builds user trust. Meanwhile, several U.S.-based companies have announced they'll restrict certain AI features in the EU market rather than pursue compliance.

"The AI Act creates a clear rulebook," said Thierry Breton, EU Commissioner for Internal Market. "Innovation thrives when there are clear rules, not when there's a regulatory vacuum."

Analysts expect the Act to influence similar legislation worldwide, much as GDPR set the global standard for data protection.
