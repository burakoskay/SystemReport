## Malicious Spyware App Targets Android Users

Researchers uncovered a government-backed operation that distributed a counterfeit Android app to plant spyware on a target phone. The app masqueraded as a legitimate utility, yet it installed surveillance code that could record calls, capture keystrokes, and exfiltrate location data.

The spyware-making firm behind the code had never before sold this class of software. The researchers did not name the company. This operation highlights the evolving nature of spyware and the methods used by government-backed entities to compromise target devices.

The use of bespoke spyware, like the one in this operation, underscores the adaptability of malicious actors. These actors continually update their tactics to evade detection and enhance their surveillance capabilities. 

## Ransomware Goes Quantum-Safe

A ransomware family has been confirmed to use post-quantum cryptography to encrypt victims' files. The family's code now incorporates algorithms designed to resist attacks from future quantum computers. This development has significant implications for cybersecurity, as it suggests that ransomware authors are preparing for a future where quantum decryption becomes feasible.

The article points out that there is "no practical benefit to use PQC" today, yet the ransomware authors have adopted it anyway. Researchers speculate that the move may be a hedge against a future where quantum decryption becomes feasible. This proactive approach by ransomware authors underscores the need for security teams to stay ahead of emerging threats.

The adoption of post-quantum cryptography by ransomware authors highlights a critical issue: the impending impact of quantum computing on current cryptographic standards. As quantum computers become more powerful, they could potentially break current encryption methods, rendering them insecure. 

## CISA Alerts on Vulnerabilities

The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) released alerts on industrial control systems and a broader catalog of known exploited vulnerabilities (KEV). The agency's latest emergency directive forces federal agencies to remediate critical flaws. This action demonstrates the growing concern over vulnerabilities in critical infrastructure and the need for swift remediation.

CISA added new critical vulnerabilities to its KEV catalog, describing them as "serious risks" to federal networks and beyond. These vulnerabilities highlight the ongoing challenge of securing complex systems and the importance of proactive measures to prevent exploitation.

The alerts issued by CISA reflect a broader effort to enhance the security posture of critical infrastructure. By identifying and addressing vulnerabilities, organizations can reduce the risk of exploitation and protect their systems from malicious actors.
