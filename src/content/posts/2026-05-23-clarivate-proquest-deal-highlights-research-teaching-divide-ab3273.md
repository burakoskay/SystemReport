## Clarivate's $5.3 B Bet on Research Data
Clarivate announced a definitive agreement to acquire ProQuest for $5.3 billion on May 17, 2021. The deal combines a $4 billion cash payment with $1.3 billion of equity, and it is expected to close in Q3 2021 pending regulatory approval. Clarivate expects the transaction to add more than $875 million in revenue and over $350 million of adjusted EBITDA after cost synergies, making the deal accretive to its diluted earnings for 2022.
Jerre Stead, Clarivate’s executive chairman and CEO, said the two businesses are “highly complementary” and share a goal to accelerate innovation through research and knowledge sharing. He added that the combined platform will give customers access to content dating back centuries and technologies that meet 21st‑century needs. ProQuest, meanwhile, serves more than 25,000 academic, corporate and research organizations in over 150 countries, delivering both content and technology solutions.
The acquisition positions Clarivate as a premier end‑to‑end research‑intelligence provider. By folding ProQuest’s data and analytics into its Research Intelligence Cloud™, Clarivate aims to create a “world‑leading software and information provider for research‑focused organizations,” according to the press release. The move also brings two ProQuest board members onto Clarivate’s board, signalling a deeper integration of leadership.

## Universities Still Play a Zero‑Sum Game Between Research and Teaching
A new multi‑part study from Nature Publishing Group surveyed 450 university faculty across more than 30 countries to probe attitudes toward research and teaching. Seventy‑seven percent said they value teaching and research equally, 16 percent placed teaching above research, and only six percent ranked research higher.
When asked to hypothetically hire a tenure‑track candidate, nearly half of the respondents chose an “exceptional researcher with no teaching experience” over a highly experienced teacher with no research background. The same respondents reported that their institutions prioritize research over teaching, with only a quarter believing their universities value both equally. The survey paints a picture of widespread inconsistency: faculty claim to value teaching, yet their hiring preferences and perceived institutional pressures tilt heavily toward research.
The authors argue that this mismatch creates a zero‑sum environment where time and resources devoted to research crowd out teaching. Since most scientific funding and prestige flow from research achievements, undergraduate education risks falling behind as faculty chase lab success over classroom impact.

## How Consolidation Fuels the Research‑First Bias
Clarivate’s purchase of ProQuest amplifies the market power of firms that aggregate and monetize research outputs. By offering a unified data cloud, the combined entity can lock in universities, publishers and corporate labs into a single subscription ecosystem. That lock‑in creates a financial incentive for institutions to produce more publishable research, because the downstream value of that research is now directly tied to the data platforms that profit from it.
The hiring paradox highlighted by the NPG survey dovetails with this market shift. When research output drives revenue for both the university and external data providers, faculty face pressure to prioritize publishable work. The acquisition’s promise of “accelerating innovation” is framed in terms of faster data access, not better teaching. In practice, the added resources from the deal are likely to flow into expanding content collections, analytics tools, and AI‑driven discovery services—areas that directly benefit research productivity.
If universities continue to align faculty incentives with the metrics that data providers reward, the teaching side of the equation will receive even less attention. The result is a feedback loop: more data, more research, more funding, and an ever‑shrinking share of institutional bandwidth for classroom improvement.

## Beyond the Headlines: Real‑World Science Still Needs Hands‑On Learning
While executives tout billions‑dollar deals and surveys expose academic contradictions, everyday scientists are still wrestling with practical challenges. This week, Engadget reported on chicks hatched from artificial eggshells as part of a new mission to study Earth’s magnetosphere. The project illustrates how niche, high‑tech experiments rely on specialized data pipelines—precisely the kind of infrastructure Clarivate now controls.
Yet the same students who will eventually run those experiments are the ones most affected by the teaching‑research imbalance. If curricula remain under‑resourced, future researchers may lack the practical skills needed to design and execute novel field studies. The magnetosphere mission, for example, demands expertise in materials science, data acquisition, and atmospheric modeling—areas that thrive on hands‑on instruction.
The stakes become clearer when you consider that the United States spends a disproportionate share of its GDP on scientific research yet underperforms on international science assessments. The NPG study suggests that an overemphasis on research output, reinforced by industry consolidation, may be a missing piece of that puzzle.

## What to Watch
Track Clarivate’s integration roadmap over the next twelve months, especially any pricing changes for university licences. Simultaneously, monitor whether faculty‑governance bodies respond to the NPG findings by revising hiring policies or workload models. A shift toward balanced evaluation metrics could temper the research‑first bias that the Clarivate‑ProQuest deal appears to reinforce. The next round of university budget allocations and tenure‑track criteria will reveal whether the industry’s push for data dominance will finally translate into better classroom outcomes.
