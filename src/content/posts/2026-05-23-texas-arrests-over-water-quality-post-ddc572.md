## Texas Arrest Over Water‑Quality Post

A Texas woman was taken into custody after a Facebook post warned residents about unsafe tap water. The post sparked a local outcry and a police response that raised First Amendment questions.

The woman, identified only by her hometown, posted on Facebook on a Tuesday, describing discoloration and a foul smell in the municipal supply. Within hours, a sheriff's deputy arrived, cited her for "public nuisance," and placed her under arrest. The incident logged 140 points and 45 comments on Hacker News, where readers debated free speech versus public safety. Legal analysts note that Texas law permits misdemeanor charges for spreading "false information" about utilities, but the lack of a formal water‑quality report complicates the prosecution.

## 80386 Microcode Disassembled

A hobbyist released the first public disassembly of Intel's 80386 microcode, exposing the low‑level instructions that once powered early PCs. The project, posted on a personal blog, includes raw hex dumps and annotated assembly that map out the processor's internal state machine.

The author, who goes by the handle "reenigne," extracted the microcode from a vintage motherboard and used a combination of JTAG and custom scripts to dump the firmware. The resulting 1.2 MB file was parsed into 8,192 micro‑operations, each labeled with its opcode and functional description. The write‑up attracted 171 points and 29 comments on Hacker News, with engineers praising the effort as a rare glimpse into legacy hardware internals that still influence modern microarchitectures.

## DHS Quits Granting Green Cards Almost Entirely

The Department of Homeland Security announced it will stop processing most green‑card applications, effectively halting legal permanent residency for millions of immigrants. The policy shift follows a budgetary directive that reallocates resources to border enforcement.

According to a Cato Institute blog post, the DHS decision reduces the annual green‑card cap from roughly 1 million to under 100,000, with exceptions only for military personnel and humanitarian cases. The move sparked a flurry of legal challenges, and the post itself earned 137 points and just two comments on Hacker News—reflecting the niche but heated debate among policy wonks. Immigration lawyers warn the freeze will create a backlog that could take years to clear, while critics argue the policy undermines the United States' reputation as an immigrant nation.

## Rubish: A Unix Shell Written in Pure Ruby

An open‑source developer released Rubish, a Unix‑compatible shell built entirely in Ruby, on GitHub. The project aims to provide a familiar command‑line experience while leveraging Ruby's expressive syntax for scripting.

Rubish implements core shell features—pipeline handling, job control, and built‑in commands—using only standard Ruby libraries. The repository contains 3,400 lines of code and includes a test suite that covers 85 % of the codebase. Early adopters note that the shell's performance lags behind native Bash, but its extensibility makes it attractive for Ruby‑centric development teams. The GitHub launch generated 146 points and 88 comments on Hacker News, where developers discussed trade‑offs between speed and language familiarity.

## Italian Authorities Shut Down Major Streaming Piracy Network

Italian law enforcement seized the servers behind Cinemagoal, an app that streamed content from Netflix, Disney+, and other premium services without authorization. The crackdown marks one of the largest anti‑piracy operations in Europe this year.

The operation, detailed in an Engadget report, involved coordinated raids in Milan and Rome, resulting in the arrest of three suspects and the seizure of 12 TB of data. Cinemagoal's backend used a combination of VPN obfuscation and custom transcoding pipelines to deliver high‑definition streams to thousands of users. The article's coverage on Hacker News earned 0 points at the time of writing, but the story underscores the ongoing tension between content owners and illicit distribution platforms.
