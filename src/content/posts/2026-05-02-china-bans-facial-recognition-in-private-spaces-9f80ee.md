## China Introduces New Facial Recognition Rules

China has introduced new rules that restrict the use of facial recognition technology in private spaces. The regulations, published by the Cyberspace Administration and Ministry of Public Security, require organizations to conduct an impact assessment before using facial recognition. This assessment must consider the necessity of using the technology, its impact on individuals' privacy, and the risks of data leakage.

The rules explicitly ban the use of facial recognition equipment in public places such as hotel rooms, public bathrooms, public dressing rooms, and public toilets. Organizations that decide to use facial recognition must encrypt biometric data, audit their information security practices, and obtain individuals' consent. The rules do not apply to researchers or to activities like machine translation of the rules, which suggests that images of citizens' faces can still be used for AI training.

## Implications and Exceptions

The new regulations do not mention whether government agencies are exempt from these rules. It's been suggested that the government may continue using facial recognition for its own purposes, such as a national identity scheme. The rules are seen as a step towards protecting individuals' privacy, but their effectiveness and scope remain to be seen.

The lack of clear guidelines on government exemptions raises concerns about potential loopholes. If government agencies are not bound by these rules, it could undermine the effectiveness of the regulations in protecting citizens' privacy. Furthermore, the rules' impact on private companies that use facial recognition technology for commercial purposes is still unclear.
