Backrooms smashed A24's opening‑day record with a $38 million haul on Friday, setting the stage for a $90 million weekend. The same day the MPAA and the National Association of Theatre Owners announced that Google Glass and other wearables are now prohibited in cinemas.

The Verge reported that Backrooms' Friday earnings eclipsed the $33.7 million day posted by *The Mandalorian* and its companion series, and blew past A24's previous best‑ever opening weekend of $25.5 million for Alex Garland's *Civil War*. With an estimated production budget of $10 million, the film's profit potential dwarfs recent blockbusters that cost upwards of $150 million. Meanwhile, the MPAA and NATO revealed the new policy at ShowEast 2014 in Hollywood, Fla., citing a January incident in Columbus, Ohio where a man wearing Google Glass during a screening of *Jack Ryan: Shadow Recruit* was questioned by Homeland Security agents.

### The immediate fallout

The ban hits a niche but vocal segment of cinema‑goers who have experimented with wearables for personal recording or augmented reality experiences. The joint statement from the MPAA and NATO emphasizes a "zero‑tolerance policy" for any recording device, echoing long‑standing rules that phones must be silenced and other devices turned off at showtime. Violators may be asked to leave, and theatre staff are instructed to alert law enforcement when they suspect illegal recording.

For studios, the policy removes a lingering legal gray area. While the industry has cracked down on smartphone recordings for years, the rise of head‑mounted displays introduced a new vector for piracy. By codifying the ban, the MPAA hopes to preempt future incidents that could jeopardize box‑office revenue, especially for high‑margin releases like *Backrooms* that rely on strong opening‑day performance.

### A century‑old precedent

The current crackdown mirrors an earlier, far more aggressive attempt to control film distribution. In December 1908, Thomas Edison convened executives from Biograph, Vitagraph, American Mutoscope and seven other studios to sign a monopolistic peace treaty that became the Motion Picture Patents Company (MPPC). Edison had already filed 23 infringement suits since his 1891 patent on a motion‑picture camera, using legal pressure to force rivals into licensing agreements.

The MPPC collected royalties, dictated which film stock could be sold (thanks to a deal with Eastman Kodak that barred non‑licensees), and effectively tried to shut out foreign competition. Although the trust briefly dominated the industry, it collapsed under antitrust scrutiny and public backlash. Historians note that the trust's downfall stemmed from its disregard for audience demand; the public's appetite for silent films in 1908 was “insatiable,” and the trust’s focus on legal control over content alienated both creators and viewers.

The Edison episode offers a cautionary parallel: legal mechanisms can temporarily suppress perceived threats, but they rarely sustain long‑term dominance when they ignore consumer preferences. The MPAA’s new wearable ban is far less sweeping than Edison’s patent empire, yet it still reflects a pattern of industry bodies turning to regulation to protect revenue.

### Technological arms race in the cinema

While the MPAA tightens rules on wearables, other tech firms are pushing new tools into the filmmaking pipeline. Nokia unveiled the Ozo VR camera at a Los Angeles event, positioning it as a professional‑grade 360‑degree capture device with eight lenses and eight microphones. The spherical body streams live footage to a VR headset, allowing on‑set monitoring—a feature that could change how directors view immersive scenes.

Chris Green of Davies Murphy Group highlighted Nokia’s decades‑long camera expertise, noting that the company had previously miniaturized high‑end optics for mobile phones. However, early reviewers described Ozo’s footage as “muddy and out of focus” in places, though its spatial audio received praise. The device targets Hollywood and media professionals rather than consumers, and Nokia expects it on sale before the end of the year, though pricing and full specs remain undisclosed.

The emergence of VR capture tools underscores why the MPAA feels compelled to act. As production equipment becomes more portable and discreet, the line between legitimate on‑set recording and illicit capture blurs. The industry’s response—whether through policy, technology, or both—will shape how theaters balance security with the evolving expectations of a tech‑savvy audience.

### Industry reactions and the road ahead

Reactions to the wearable ban have been mixed. Theatre owners argue that the rule protects a $100 billion global box‑office ecosystem, while privacy advocates warn that blanket prohibitions may alienate early adopters who view wearables as extensions of personal media consumption. Some independent cinemas have already experimented with “no‑phone” policies, but enforcing a ban on head‑mounted devices presents logistical challenges.

Meanwhile, *Backrooms*’ runaway success demonstrates that blockbuster‑level earnings are still possible for low‑budget films, especially when they tap into niche horror fandoms. The film’s performance may encourage studios to greenlight more modest projects, shifting some focus away from mega‑budget spectacles that have struggled at the box office.

The convergence of a record‑breaking opening, a renewed anti‑piracy stance, and emerging VR capture technology paints a picture of an industry at a crossroads. Studios must navigate profit‑driven incentives, protect intellectual property, and adapt to new production tools—all while keeping audiences engaged.

### What to watch

Track how theaters enforce the wearable ban during the upcoming holiday season, when attendance spikes and the temptation to record rises. Monitor whether any lawsuits challenge the MPAA/NATO policy on First Amendment grounds. On the production side, watch for early adopters of Nokia’s Ozo on upcoming releases; its performance could dictate whether VR capture becomes a standard part of the filmmaking toolkit. Finally, keep an eye on box‑office trends after *Backrooms*—if other low‑budget titles replicate its opening, studios may recalibrate their green‑light criteria.
