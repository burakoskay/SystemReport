## Conviction in Landmark Aviation Case

Air France and Airbus were found guilty of manslaughter by a French court on Wednesday in connection with the 2009 crash of an Airbus A330-200 that killed all 228 people on board.

The crash occurred on June 1, 2009, involving Air France Flight 447, an Airbus A330-200 en route from Rio de Janeiro to Paris. The French Bureau of Enquiry and Analysis for Civil Aviation Safety (BEA) concluded that pitot-tube icing and loss of airspeed data led to the disaster.

The BEA's investigation revealed that inadequate crew response to the aircraft's stall warnings was a critical factor. The court also heard that the pilots' actions during the critical phase of the flight were flawed.

## Technical and Human Factors

The investigation by BEA identified multiple factors contributing to the crash. Pitot tubes, which measure airspeed, became blocked by ice, leading to incorrect readings. This caused the autopilot to disconnect and the aircraft to stall. Crew procedures and training were also criticized.

Airbus had previously redesigned the pitot tubes to improve resistance to icing. However, the redesign did not prevent the accident. The court found that both Air France and Airbus had a responsibility to ensure the aircraft was airworthy.

The redesign of the pitot tubes was part of a series of upgrades to the A330's air data system. These upgrades were intended to improve the aircraft's performance in icing conditions. However, the investigation revealed that the upgrades did not go far enough.
