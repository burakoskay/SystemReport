Apple’s 15‑inch M5 MacBook Air is now $200 off on Amazon, while a new AI sales assistant and interview simulator launched on Hacker News. The price cuts make high‑end hardware cheaper, and the software tools promise to automate routine work for engineers and sales teams.

9to5Mac reported that the base 15‑inch M5 MacBook Air starts at $590 with a $200 discount, and the premium model tops out at $270 off. AirPods 4 sell for about 25% less, and the AirPods Max 2 hit an all‑time low price. The same article also highlighted a 40% discount on Apple’s iPhone Air MagSafe Battery. These deals arrived alongside two Show HN posts: one for AutoCRM, an AI‑powered sales tracking agent, and another for Mockmaster, a multilingual interview simulation platform.

## Apple hardware hits Amazon price floor

The discount on the 15‑inch M5 MacBook Air is the deepest seen since the model’s launch. Buyers can ship the base configuration in one day, a perk that usually costs extra. The $270 discount applies to the top‑tier configuration, which includes a larger SSD and higher‑end GPU. Apple’s AirPods Max 2, released earlier this year, are now listed at the lowest Amazon price ever recorded, according to the deal roundup.

The price reductions come as Apple’s supply chain stabilizes after a year of component shortages. Retailers are using aggressive markdowns to clear inventory before the holiday season. For power users, the combination of a discounted M5 MacBook Air and cheap accessories creates a compelling upgrade path without breaking the budget.

## AutoCRM automates the sales grind

A developer posted AutoCRM to Hacker News, describing it as an “intelligent teammate” that monitors emails and WhatsApp, creates contacts, updates deal stages, and logs calls without user intervention. The tool claims to eliminate the manual steps of logging a prospect call, updating the pipeline, and scheduling follow‑ups. It also offers a “Safety Filter” that removes spam and sensitive data before processing.

AutoCRM’s privacy‑first architecture means the AI never stores raw communications on external servers. Users can see exactly what the model analyzed and why it suggested a particular action, avoiding black‑box behavior. The service launches with a free 14‑day trial and no credit‑card requirement, positioning itself against established CRMs that rely on manual data entry.

Early adopters on the Hacker News thread praised the reduction in busywork but warned that the system’s reliance on email parsing could miss context in informal messaging. The developer promises regular updates and an open‑source component for the safety filter, aiming to build trust among sales teams wary of AI‑driven data handling.

## Mockmaster brings AI interview practice to any language

Another Show HN post introduced Mockmaster, an interview simulation tool that generates role‑specific questions in any programming language. The platform can pose a Google‑style A/B test scenario, ask for JavaScript implementations of graph algorithms, or probe disaster‑recovery strategies for mission‑critical services. After each mock interview, Mockmaster provides feedback on answer completeness and time‑complexity analysis.

The tool’s breadth covers front‑end performance debugging, distributed‑system design, and statistical testing for A/B experiments. Users can select a language and receive code‑level prompts, then compare their solution against an AI‑generated reference. Mockmaster markets itself as a confidence builder for engineers preparing for technical interviews across sectors.

Feedback from early users highlights the realistic pacing of the simulated interview and the detailed explanations of why certain approaches are preferred. However, some participants noted that the AI sometimes offers overly generic feedback, missing nuanced trade‑offs that senior engineers would discuss.

## The AI productivity arms race

AutoCRM and Mockmaster join a crowded field of AI‑enhanced productivity tools that promise to replace manual data entry, scheduling, and skill rehearsal. Companies like Notion, ClickUp, and Salesforce have integrated large‑language‑model assistants into their platforms, blurring the line between software and coworker.

The surge raises questions about data security, especially when tools ingest private communications. AutoCRM’s safety filter attempts to address this, but regulators in the EU and US are still drafting guidelines for AI that processes personal data. Meanwhile, the market’s rapid expansion creates a winner‑takes‑most dynamic; platforms that can demonstrate reliable accuracy and transparent decision‑making are likely to attract enterprise contracts.

For engineers, the influx of AI interview simulators like Mockmaster could shift hiring practices. Recruiters may start expecting candidates to demonstrate proficiency with AI‑generated prompts, while candidates could rely on these tools to rehearse responses. This feedback loop could accelerate the adoption of AI in both hiring and daily workflow.

## What to watch

Track Apple’s discount cadence through the holiday quarter; a deeper price cut could signal inventory pressure ahead of new product releases. Monitor AutoCRM’s user growth and any enterprise case studies that validate its claim of “zero manual pipeline updates.” Finally, watch for Mockmaster’s expansion into corporate training programs, which could turn interview practice into a subscription revenue stream. The next quarter will reveal whether these AI tools earn a foothold or fade amid the crowded productivity market.
